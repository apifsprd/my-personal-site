The world is captivated by a city in a mountainous region often shrouded in fog, with extreme temperatures that can reach 43 degrees Celsius. Yet, that challenging geography has not hindered the construction of massive bridges, skyscrapers, and even a state-of-the-art monorail that cuts through buildings like a scene from the movie 'Cyberpunk.' That is Chongqing, a metropolis on par with Beijing and Shanghai.

We also witness how China’s high-speed rail network—which in the early 2000s still resembled fragmented, disconnected projects—has now spread like a complex spider’s web and become the lifeblood of the nation’s economy. The same goes for the Islamic Republic of Iran; despite being shackled by suffocating economic sanctions, they have proven capable of standing tall and competing on the global stage of technology and education.

The marvels described above are not merely a matter of technological superiority, but a manifestation of an uninterrupted command system. Here, decisions are made overnight and executed without compromise. This phenomenon raises a troubling question: has the democracy we hold in such high regard actually become a brake on progress?

### When Democracy Loses Its Way

A stark contrast emerges when we turn our gaze toward nations that have made democracy their lifeblood. In the United States, we witness how acute political polarization has transformed the legislative chamber into an arena of endless debate. Public policy is often held hostage by partisan interests. We see how the Trump era brought massive disruption—from aggressive attempts to intervene in Venezuela’s sovereignty to secure resources, to the escalation of tensions with Iran that ultimately hit American citizens through soaring energy prices. Here, the checks and balances mechanism, which should serve as a safeguard, often grinds to a halt amid aging infrastructure and a widening economic divide.

The reality in Indonesia is not much different. The ritual of elections every five years actually creates a new trap: a narrow-minded policy orientation that only pursues instant results for the sake of re-election. As a result, strategic development is often neglected or forced through without sound long-term planning. Amid the weakening of party institutions and corruption that continues to erode the system, our democracy is trapped in unproductive “noise.” We resemble a giant trying to run, yet its legs are bound by the chains of bureaucracy and conflicting interests. In this country, deliberation—which should serve as a compass for finding a way out—all too often becomes a labyrinth that leads development into uncertainty.

Democracies across the globe are facing similar pathologies: acute polarization, systemic corruption, and political parties that have become mere vehicles for personal ambition. Amid this noise, minorities are increasingly sidelined, underscoring the deeply unequal face of modern democracy.

### A Spark from Torres

However, a spark of revolution has emerged from an unexpected place: Torres, a city in western Venezuela with a population of 200,000 that has been gripped by local elites for centuries. There, the residents elected an “unknown” candidate named Julio Chavez. His vision is radical yet simple: to return power to the people. He will no longer allow budgets and policies to be decided behind bureaucratic desks. “In the past, officials sat in air-conditioned rooms all day without ever setting foot in our neighborhoods. Who knows our needs better: those who stay in their offices, or us who live in this community?” a resident remarked bitterly.

### Porto Alegre and the Manifestation of Civilized Democracy

The spirit in Torres is just a small part of the grand narrative unfolding in Porto Alegre, Brazil. In this city, located 2,100 km from the capital Brasília, a quarter of the city’s budget is entirely entrusted to the will of its citizens. A decade later, this system has evolved into a global phenomenon—from New York to Seville and Hamburg. This is what we know as Participatory Budgeting, or as we often call it in our country, Musrenbang. A movement born from a simple idea: a civilized dialogue between leaders and citizens. It may sound boring to some, but for a democracy that is ailing, this is the most miraculous remedy.

The magic in Torres and Porto Alegre didn’t come from piles of reports, but from the courage to break down barriers. When a leader steps out of an air-conditioned room and stands on muddy ground or in front of a clogged drain, they’re no longer just reading data—they’re directly experiencing their citizens’ hardships.

The dialogue that takes place is no longer merely a bureaucratic formality, but a process of “feeling” that accelerates the movement of democracy. The stagnation of democracy has often been caused by a cognitive gap that is too wide between decision-makers and the subjects of their decisions. However, when policies emerge from discussions on residents’ front porches or in sweltering village halls, the obstacles of convoluted bureaucracy and public distrust dissolve instantly. Democracy, which was once slow due to “mutual distrust,” suddenly races forward because of the direct legitimacy derived from the sweat and voices of its own people.

### Conclusion

Perhaps we have misjudged. Democracy is not lagging behind because it is slow; it is lagging behind because it has lost its human touch. Chongqing shows us the grandeur of a command-and-control system, but Porto Alegre reminds us that when the people are involved, democracy does not merely function—it can soar higher without sacrificing the dignity of its citizens
