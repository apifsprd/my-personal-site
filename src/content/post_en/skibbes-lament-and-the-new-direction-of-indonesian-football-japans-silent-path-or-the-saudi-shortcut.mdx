The lights at Alinma Bank Stadium in King Abdullah Sports City, Jeddah, had not yet fully dimmed when Michael Skibbe stepped into the press conference room with a grim expression. The coach of Vissel Kobe—the J-League giant that was once the magical home of Andres Iniesta—had just been forced to kneel by the hosts, Al-Ahli. However, what caused Skibbe’s lament was not merely the numbers on the scoreboard or Kobe's elimination from the Asian Champions League Elite (ACL Elite) semifinals; it was the bitter reality of an "absurd quality difference" between clubs in East and West Asia.

Before him, Skibbe didn't just face a football team; he faced a "lighthouse project" populated by big names who had recently departed the glitz of Europe: Ivan Toney (formerly of Brentford in the Premier League), Riyad Mahrez (ex-Man City), Franck Kessie (ex-AC Milan), Merih Demiral (ex-Juventus), and Edouard Mendy (ex-Chelsea).

This contrast was made more painful when looking at the Vissel Kobe squad, which is dominated by Japanese National Team veterans returning home in the twilight of their careers: Takashi Inui (ex-Eibar, 37), the hero of Russia 2018; Gotoku Sakai (ex-Hamburg, 35); Yuya Osako (ex-Werder Bremen, 35); and Yoshinori Muto (ex-Newcastle United, 33).

"This big gap between the quality of the football in eastern Asia and western Asia is because the clubs in Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and so on are much more powerful; they have much more money in the leagues," Skibbe lamented, as quoted by Reuters.

Skibbe’s complaint is not merely the excuse of a loser; it is a loud alarm for Asian football—and perhaps a compass for Indonesia, which is currently searching for its footballing identity.

### Japan’s Silent Path: Loyal to Structure

Football in the Land of the Rising Sun is known for highly ambitious long-term projects, planned to span a century. Since the J-League launched in 1993, Japan has established a "100-Year Plan" vision that focuses not just on winning on the pitch, but on building a football culture rooted in every community. For them, football is not about winning trophies overnight, but about building a healthy ecosystem organically.

The path Japan has taken is a "silent path" that demands extraordinary patience. Instead of burning money to pay world-class stars in their prime—as Saudi Arabian clubs do—Japanese clubs choose to bow to the supremacy of structure and financial sustainability. Club budgets are precisely allocated to youth academies and world-class training facilities. When star players do arrive, they usually come in their veteran years; they are brought in not just for instant victories, but to transmit professionalism to local talents.

Success in Japan is not measured by how many stars they import, but by how many young talents they successfully "export" to top European leagues. This was clearly reflected in the Japan National Team squad list during the March-April 2026 FIFA calendar. Out of 29 names, only three players came from the domestic league—two of whom are goalkeepers Keisuke Osako (Sanfrecce Hiroshima) and Tomoki Hayakawa (Kashima Antlers), plus Ryunosuke Sato (FC Tokyo). The rest are "expats" carving careers at elite European clubs like Kaoru Mitoma (Brighton/PL), Daichi Kamada (Crystal Palace/PL), and Takehiro Tomiyasu (Ajax/Eredivisie).

Japan is willing to let its domestic league lose its best players for the long-term progress of the National Team. However, Michael Skibbe’s grievance in Jeddah reveals the dark side of this philosophical choice: when decades of loyalty to a process collide with a "shortcut" in the form of an instant wall of money, the believer in the process often has to go home with their head bowed.

### The Saudi Shortcut: Buying the Future

In contrast to Japan’s patience, Saudi Arabia has chosen a much more aggressive and instant path: massive capital disruption. Since June 2023, backed by unlimited funds from the Sovereign Wealth Fund (PIF)—a giant investment institution similar to Indonesia's "Danantara"—Saudi Arabia has not had the luxury of waiting a century to reach elite standards. They chose to close that gap by bringing the highest standards of world football, along with its protagonists, directly to the Middle East.

This is a legitimate "shortcut" in modern sports economics. Instead of focusing on exporting talent to Europe, the Saudi Pro League does the opposite: they "import" Europe to their own backyard. The presence of Riyad Mahrez, Ivan Toney, and Franck Kessie at Al-Ahli is just the tip of the iceberg. Elsewhere, there are Karim Benzema, Fabinho, and Steven Bergwijn at Al-Ittihad; and megastar Cristiano Ronaldo at Al-Nassr, now surrounded by names of the caliber of Joao Felix, Kingsley Coman, and Sadio Mane. There is no need to elaborate on their track records; you already know they are names still highly capable of playing in Europe's top leagues.

Their presence is not just an effort to win a single match in the ACL Elite, but a mission to create a new axis in Asian football. By bringing in world players in their prime, the standard of the domestic competition is forced to rise radically overnight. Saudi Arabia believes in the theory of acceleration: their local players will develop much faster if they have to fight for the ball every day with former Premier League or Serie A champions, rather than playing in a "comfortable" but mediocre league.

They are not building a system slowly; they are buying time. Skibbe’s complaint in Jeddah is proof that this "shortcut" has successfully created a tangible wall of quality. For Saudi Arabia, if money can buy quality capable of tearing down a 100-year system in just 90 minutes, then the investment has been paid in full.

### Seeking Indonesia’s Football Compass

Michael Skibbe’s lament in Jeddah ultimately serves as a large mirror for Indonesian football. Currently, we seem to be standing at a crossroads, trying to pluck the fruits of both models simultaneously. On one hand, PSSI is taking a "shortcut" similar to the Saudi spirit through the naturalization of diaspora players to instantly raise the National Team's standards. The results are starting to show; we are no longer just making up the numbers in Asia.

This acceleration effort has also bled into the domestic competition. Through the latest regulations, Liga 1 clubs are now allowed to register up to 11 foreign players. Although only a maximum of 9 players can be on the match sheet and only 7 can be on the pitch at once, this policy clearly demonstrates an instant ambition: to forcibly improve the quality of the competition so that Indonesian clubs can compete on the Asian stage without waiting for the long process of local talent development.

However, on the other hand, we are still stumbling to find the "silent path" like Japan. Improving league structures, strict club licensing, and sustainable grassroots development remain "homework" that is often less sexy than the euphoria of temporary victories. We want the lightning-fast achievements characteristic of Saudi Arabia, but with capital constraints that demand the extra patience characteristic of Japan.

The question is, which direction will we take? Indonesia may not have unlimited funds like Saudi’s "Danantara" to buy world standards on a massive scale, but we also do not have the luxury of waiting a century like Japan.

Perhaps, our football's new compass is not about choosing one, but about finding a balance: using the "shortcut" to build dignity at the international level, while diligently building the "silent path" at the domestic level. Because, in the end, a civilized football is one that knows when to run fast to catch up, and when to pause to build a foundation that will not crumble with the changing times.
